# Jazz Piano

Jazz piano is a keyboard performance style rooted in the African American musical traditions of the early twentieth century, blending blues, ragtime, and European harmonic influences into a uniquely flexible idiom. Pioneering figures such as Jelly Roll Morton and Art Tatum established foundational approaches including stride piano and virtuosic reharmonization, while Thelonious Monk — rather than following bebop's fluid, running-line convention — developed a deliberately angular style characterized by dissonant intervals, sustained silences, and rhythmically displaced phrasing that treated the piano percussively and melodically at once. The instrument occupies a central role in jazz ensembles, simultaneously fulfilling harmonic, rhythmic, and melodic functions through techniques such as comping, walking bass lines, and solo improvisation. Bill Evans extended the instrument's expressive range by introducing impressionistic, close-voiced chord voicings and a lyrical approach to trio interplay, recorded most influentially on Miles Davis's 1959 album *Kind of Blue*. Evans's harmonic vocabulary and interactive trio concept directly shaped subsequent generations of jazz pianists, including Keith Jarrett, Brad Mehldau, and Hiromi Uehara, making his stylistic influence on contemporary jazz piano both traceable and well-documented.
