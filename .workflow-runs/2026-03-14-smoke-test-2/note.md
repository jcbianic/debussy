Jazz piano is a style of piano playing rooted in African American musical traditions that emerged in New Orleans in the early 20th century. It encompasses a wide range of substyles—from the stride piano of Fats Waller and James P. Johnson to the bebop innovations of Bud Powell and the modal explorations of Bill Evans. A defining characteristic is improvisation, where the pianist spontaneously composes melodic lines and harmonic variations over a chord progression. The style has continued to evolve through free jazz, where pioneers like Cecil Taylor abandoned conventional harmony and meter in favor of total improvisational freedom, and into contemporary forms that blend jazz with electronic music, hip-hop, and world music traditions—as heard in the work of artists such as Vijay Iyer and Robert Glasper.
